# Cancellation & Refund Policy – Grand Plaza Hotel

**Last updated:** January 2024  
**Owner:** Guest Services Team  
**Document ID:** POL-GS-2024-001

## Overview

This document outlines how cancellations and refunds are handled across different booking types at Grand Plaza Hotel. The policy has been designed to balance guest flexibility with operational requirements.

In general, the cancellation terms depend on three main factors: the rate type selected at booking, how close the cancellation is to the scheduled check-in date, and the booking channel used (direct booking vs online travel agency).

Guests are strongly advised to review their booking confirmation email for exact terms, as some exceptions and special conditions may apply based on seasonal demand, group bookings, or corporate agreements.

---

## Standard Bookings (Flexible Rate)

For most direct bookings made through our website, reservation center, or front desk, the standard cancellation window is **48 hours prior to check-in**. The cutoff time is 2:00 PM local time on the day that falls 48 hours before the scheduled arrival.

If a guest cancels their reservation more than 48 hours before the scheduled check-in time, they will receive a full refund of any prepaid amounts. The refund will be processed to the original payment method within 5-7 business days.

If a guest cancels within the 48-hour window, a penalty of one night's room rate plus applicable taxes will be charged. This charge is applied to cover the operational costs and lost revenue opportunity, as last-minute cancellations make it difficult to resell the room.

In cases where a guest fails to arrive and does not cancel the reservation (commonly referred to as a "no-show"), the full stay amount may be charged. However, in practice, most no-show situations result in a charge equivalent to one night's rate unless the reservation falls during peak season or special events.

For example, if a guest has a reservation for Friday at 2:00 PM, the free cancellation deadline would be Wednesday at 2:00 PM. Cancellations made after this time would incur the one-night penalty.

In practice, front desk staff may exercise discretion to allow minor flexibility of a few hours depending on current occupancy levels. This is particularly true during periods of lower demand when the hotel has sufficient inventory to accommodate last-minute bookings.

### Modifications to Existing Reservations

Date changes are generally accommodated if requested within the 48-hour cancellation window. The hotel will make reasonable efforts to honor the original rate for new dates, subject to availability. If the new dates fall within six months of the original booking and rooms are available, modifications are typically processed without penalty.

Room type changes follow a similar approach. Upgrades to higher room categories are straightforward, with guests paying the difference in rate. Downgrades to lower room categories are more complex due to refund processing requirements and are best handled at the time of check-in when possible.

## Non-Refundable Bookings

Certain discounted rates are offered as **non-refundable** bookings. These rates provide guests with significant savings, typically ranging from 15% to 25% below the standard flexible rate, in exchange for a commitment to the reservation.

When a guest books a non-refundable rate, the full payment is charged immediately at the time of booking. These reservations cannot be cancelled or modified under normal circumstances. The non-refundable nature of these bookings is clearly indicated during the booking process, in the rate name, and in the confirmation email.

In rare and exceptional cases, such as documented medical emergencies or other compassionate circumstances, exceptions may be reviewed on a case-by-case basis by management. However, guests should not expect automatic approval of such exceptions.

It is important to note that "change of plans" or "found a better rate" are not considered valid reasons for exception requests on non-refundable bookings. The discounted rate is offered specifically because of the commitment and reduced flexibility.

## Refund Processing and Timelines

Refunds are not processed instantaneously and the timeline varies depending on the payment method used for the original booking.

For credit card payments made directly to the hotel, refunds typically appear in the guest's account within 5 to 7 business days from the date the cancellation is processed. This timeline accounts for payment gateway processing and the guest's banking institution's posting schedule. It is important to note that these are business days, not calendar days, and the count begins from when the hotel processes the refund, not from when the guest initiates the cancellation.

For bookings made through online travel agencies (OTAs) such as Booking.com or Expedia, the refund timeline can extend to 10 to 14 business days. This extended timeline is due to the additional processing layer, as the OTA serves as the merchant of record and must process the refund through their own systems before it reaches the guest's account.

For corporate billing arrangements where charges are invoiced monthly, refunds are typically applied as credits on the next billing cycle. A credit memo is usually issued within 3 business days of the cancellation being processed.

Delays in refund processing often occur due to payment gateway processing times rather than hotel action. Guests who inquire about refunds within the first few days after cancellation are usually still within the normal processing window.

### Refund Method Restrictions

Refunds can only be processed to the original payment method used for the booking. This is a standard fraud prevention measure across the hospitality industry. Even if a guest has lost or cancelled the original credit card, the refund must still be directed to that card account, and the guest's banking institution will handle the credit appropriately.

The only exception to this rule occurs when the original payment was made using a virtual card number, which is common in corporate booking scenarios. In such cases, the hotel may need to issue a refund via check, which requires escalation to the accounting department.

## Common Guest Issues and Resolutions

Based on recurring guest inquiries and complaints logged in our guest services system, several common misunderstandings have been identified.

### "I cancelled but still got charged"

This complaint typically arises when a guest cancels within the penalty window (less than 48 hours before check-in) and is surprised to see a charge on their statement. In most cases, the guest either misunderstood the cancellation deadline or did not realize they were within the penalty period.

When addressing this concern, staff should verify the exact timestamp of the cancellation in the property management system, confirm the scheduled check-in date and time, and review the rate type that was booked. If the cancellation occurred at 47 hours before check-in, it falls within the penalty window and the charge is valid per the stated policy.

The booking confirmation email, which clearly states the cancellation policy, serves as the primary reference document in these situations.

### "Refund not received yet"

This inquiry usually occurs when a guest expects an immediate refund and contacts the hotel within a few days of cancellation. In most cases, the refund is still within the normal processing window.

Staff should verify when the cancellation was processed, confirm the payment method used, and determine whether more than 7 business days have elapsed. If the timeline has been exceeded, the matter should be escalated to the accounting department to investigate potential payment gateway issues.

### "Charged twice"

This concern frequently arises from confusion between authorization holds and actual charges. When a booking is made, the payment processor places an authorization hold on the guest's card, which appears as a pending charge. When the actual charge is processed, guests may see both the hold and the charge temporarily, leading to the perception of being charged twice.

The authorization hold typically drops off within 3 to 5 business days and is not an actual charge. However, if a guest is seeing two actual posted charges (not authorization holds), this represents a genuine problem that requires immediate escalation and investigation.

## Exception Handling for Special Circumstances

Certain situations warrant consideration outside the standard cancellation policy. The hotel recognizes that genuine emergencies and unforeseen circumstances may prevent guests from honoring their reservations.

Examples of circumstances that may qualify for exception review include documented medical emergencies requiring hospitalization, flight cancellations caused by airline operational issues (not passenger choice), natural disasters affecting travel ability, death of an immediate family member, and mandatory jury duty.

When a guest requests an exception, the hotel typically asks for supporting documentation. This may include a doctor's note or hospital admission paperwork for medical emergencies, airline cancellation confirmation for flight issues, death certificates or obituaries for bereavement situations, or court summons for jury duty.

The request for documentation should be handled with appropriate sensitivity and professionalism. Staff should express empathy while explaining that documentation is required for record-keeping and policy compliance purposes.

Once documentation is reviewed by management, refunds may be issued as full refunds (most common for genuine emergencies), partial refunds (if the guest can still travel but needs to shorten their stay), or converted to future stay credits (if the guest prefers to rebook at a later date).

Manager discretion allows for refunds up to $500 without requiring director-level approval. Exceptions involving amounts exceeding $500 must be escalated to the Director of Operations for review and approval.

## Online Travel Agency (OTA) Bookings

Bookings made through third-party platforms such as Booking.com, Expedia, Hotels.com, and similar services are subject to special handling procedures. This is a significant source of guest confusion and requires clear communication.

For OTA bookings, cancellations must be processed through the same platform where the booking was made. The refund is processed by the OTA, not directly by the hotel. This is because the OTA serves as the merchant of record and handles all payment processing.

A common scenario involves a guest who books through an OTA but then contacts the hotel directly to request cancellation. While hotel staff can view the booking in the property management system, they cannot cancel it or process refunds. The guest must return to the OTA's website or contact the OTA's customer service to make any changes.

The reason for this restriction is that the OTA, not the hotel, collected the payment and maintains the contractual relationship with the guest for booking purposes. The hotel's role is limited to providing the accommodation itself.

Hotel staff can confirm that a booking exists, explain the general cancellation policy, and provide the OTA's contact information. However, they cannot modify the reservation, process refunds, or make exceptions to the OTA's specific terms.

In extreme cases where an OTA is unresponsive and a guest is physically present at the hotel with a legitimate concern, the situation may be escalated to management for potential manual intervention. However, such actions create accounting complications and should be avoided when possible.

## Group Reservations (10 or more rooms)

Group bookings follow different cancellation terms that are typically negotiated on a case-by-case basis depending on the size of the group, season, and specific requirements.

Standard group terms, when no custom contract exists, generally follow this structure: cancellations made 30 or more days before arrival receive a full refund minus a $50 administrative fee; cancellations made 15 to 29 days before arrival receive a 50% refund; and cancellations made less than 15 days before arrival receive no refund.

Many group contracts also include an attrition clause, which requires the group to fill a minimum percentage (typically 80%) of their blocked rooms. If the group fails to meet this threshold, they may be charged for the unfilled rooms at 50% of the contracted rate.

It is important to note that group terms are highly variable and are documented in individual group contracts. Staff should always reference the specific contract in the system before making any commitments or providing information to group organizers.

---

## Corporate Account Bookings

Corporate bookings often have special terms negotiated as part of broader corporate travel agreements. These terms may differ significantly from standard policies.

When no custom corporate contract exists, standard corporate terms typically include a 24-hour cancellation window (more flexible than the standard 48-hour policy), a no-show fee of $75 or 50% of the first night's rate (whichever is less), and monthly billing where cancellation fees appear on the monthly invoice rather than being charged immediately.

However, many large corporate clients have negotiated custom terms that may be even more flexible. Staff should always verify whether a custom corporate contract exists before communicating cancellation terms to corporate bookers.

## Extended Stay Reservations (7 or more nights)

Reservations spanning seven or more consecutive nights are subject to modified cancellation terms that recognize the longer commitment period.

For extended stays, cancellations made 7 or more days before arrival receive a full refund. Cancellations made 3 to 6 days before arrival result in a charge for the first night. Cancellations made less than 48 hours before arrival result in a charge for two nights.

Additionally, if a guest checks in but departs earlier than originally planned, they may be charged for 50% of the remaining nights, with a minimum charge of one night. However, early departure penalties are rarely enforced except during peak season periods, and staff are encouraged to use discretion based on circumstances.

## Loyalty Program Member Benefits

Members of the Grand Plaza Hotel loyalty program receive enhanced cancellation benefits based on their tier status. These benefits are designed to reward frequent guests with additional flexibility.

Gold Status members, who have stayed 25 or more nights in the past year, receive a 24-hour cancellation window instead of the standard 48 hours. They also have their no-show fee waived once per calendar year.

Platinum Status members, who have stayed 50 or more nights in the past year, receive a 12-hour cancellation window and have their no-show fee waived twice per calendar year. They also receive guaranteed late arrival without prior notification.

Diamond Status members, who have stayed 75 or more nights in the past year, can cancel up until check-in time without penalty. Their no-show fees are waived unlimited times. Additionally, if a Diamond member is unsatisfied with their room upon check-in, they may receive a full refund for the first night.

These enhanced benefits reflect the significant value that loyal guests bring to the hotel and staff should make every effort to accommodate requests from high-tier loyalty members.

## Internal Operational Guidelines

Staff members handling cancellations and refunds should adhere to several key operational guidelines to ensure consistency and avoid escalations.

Always verify the booking source before making any commitments about refunds. Check the exact cancellation timestamp in the property management system rather than relying on the guest's recollection. Document all guest communications in the system notes for future reference. Maintain empathy and professionalism while remaining firm on policy requirements.

Do not commit to refund timelines that exceed the stated policy. Avoid phrases like "you'll have your refund in 3 days" when the policy states 5-7 business days. Do not process refunds exceeding $500 without manager approval. Never attempt to cancel OTA bookings directly through the hotel system.

Escalation procedures should be followed for specific situations: refund disputes involving amounts over $500 should be escalated to a manager; angry guests threatening social media action should be escalated to a manager; potential chargeback situations should be escalated to both a manager and the accounting department; and any legal threats should be escalated to a manager and the Director of Operations.

Past incidents have demonstrated the importance of accurate communication. Incorrect commitments regarding refund timelines or amounts have resulted in guest complaints on social media platforms, which create reputational challenges for the hotel.

## Peak Season and Holiday Modifications

During peak travel periods, including the winter holiday season (December 20 through January 5) and major local events, the hotel may implement modified cancellation terms to account for high demand and limited inventory.

Peak season modifications typically include an extended cancellation window of 7 days, no-show penalties equal to the full reservation amount, and minimum stay requirements (usually 2 to 3 nights). These modifications are clearly communicated at the time of booking and are included in the confirmation email.

---

## Credit Card Chargeback Situations

When a guest disputes a charge with their credit card company, initiating what is known as a chargeback, the hotel must respond with appropriate documentation to defend the charge.

The key to successfully defending chargebacks is maintaining clear documentation. This includes the booking confirmation that shows the guest agreed to the cancellation policy, the exact timestamp of any cancellation (if applicable), all email communications with the guest, and detailed notes in the property management system.

Evidence must be submitted to the accounting department within 7 days of receiving the chargeback notification. Hotels typically prevail in chargeback disputes when they can demonstrate that the guest was clearly informed of and agreed to the cancellation terms at the time of booking.
